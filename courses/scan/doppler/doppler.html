DOPPLER PHYSICS

Based on the Doppler Effect.

Frequency changes when sound reflects from moving objects. Example: blood cells.

Exceed → aliasing

DOPPLER EFFECT

Frequency shift of returning echoes caused by moving structures (e.g., red blood cells). Or Doppler measures frequency shift caused by moving blood based on Doppler Effect.

DOPPLER SHIFT

Is the difference between the transmitted frequency and the received frequency.

Depends on:

  • Blood velocity
  • Doppler angle
  • Frequency
  • Speed of sound

TYPES OF DOPPLER SHIFT

  1. POSITIVE SHIFT

    2. Frequency moving towards the transducer. And are represented above the baseline.

  2. NEGATIVE SHIFT

    3. Frequency moving away the transducer. And are represented below the baseline.

TYPES OF DOPPLER

  1. SPECTRAL DOPPLER

    TYPES OF SPECTRAL DOPPLER

    There are two types of spectral doppler

    1. PULSED WAVE DOPPLER

      Collects doppler information from a small area (the sample gate). There is solitary ultrasound crystal that both sends and receives the signals.

    2. CONTINUOUS WAVE DOPPLER

      There is two ultrasound crystal, one that sends and one that receives the signals.

  2. COLOR DOPPLER

    Can detect presence or absence of flow, flow velocity and direction of flow

    Colors represent:

    • Red = toward probe
    • Blue = away from probe

  3. POWER DOPPLER

    It detects present or absence of blood flow. It detects the strength of the doppler signal returning to the transducer rather than the shift in frequency.

DOPPLER MEASUREMENTS

  1. PSV (Peak systolic velocity)
  2. EDV (End diastolic velocity)
  3. Resistive Index

    4. RI = (PSV - EDV) / PSV

  4. Pulsatility Index

    5. PI = (PSV - EDV) / Mean

DOPPLER ARTIFACTS

ALIASING

Occurs in pulsed wave doppler, color doppler but does not occur in continuous wave doppler. Aliasing happens because pulsed Doppler samples intermittently. If velocity > Nyquist, signal wraps around baseline.

Fix by:

  • Increase PRF
  • Decrease frequency
  • Lower baseline
  • Use continuous wave Doppler

SPECTRAL BROADENING

Waveform becomes thick

Causes::

  • Turbulent flow
  • Large gate
  • High gain

DOPPLER SETTINGS

When your Doppler waveform is not correct, too small, aliased, noisy, or cut off, these are the main controls you adjust:

  1. SCALE (PRF - PULSE REPETITION FREQUENCY)

    What it controls:

    • The maximum velocity you can measure before aliasing.
    • Display height of the waveform

    When to adjust:

    • If waveform is cut off at the top/bottom → Increase PRF
    • If waveform is too small and flat → Decrease PRF

    Clinical Tip:

    • High velocities (e.g., carotid stenosis) → High PRF
    • Low velocities (e.g., portal vein) → Low PRF

  2. BASELINE SHIFT

    What it controls:

    • Moves the waveform up or down on the display.

    When to adjust:

    • If waveform is hitting one side but you want more space.
    • Helps reduce aliasing appearance

    NB. Baseline does NOT change velocity — it only changes display position.

  3. DOPPLER ANGLE (ANGLE CORRECTION)

    This is VERY IMPORTANT.

    What to do:

    • Keep angle ≤ 60°
    • Align angle correction line parallel to blood flow

    If angle is wrong:

    • Velocity reading will be falsely high or low.

  4. GAIN (SPECTRAL DOPPLER GAIN)

    Adjust when:

    • Waveform looks faint → Increase gain
    • Background noise filling window → Decrease gain

    Correct gain = clear waveform without excessive noise.

  5. WALL FILTER

    What it removes:

    • Low-frequency signals from vessel wall motion.

    Adjust when:

    • Low velocities are being cut off → Lower wall filter
    • Too much low-frequency noise → Increase wall filter

    Examples:

    • Portal vein → Low wall filter
    • Arterial high-flow → Higher wall filter

  6. SAMPLE VOLUME (GATE) SIZE & POSITION

    Gate size:

    • Large gate → more signals, more spectral broadening
    • Small gate → cleaner waveform

    Gate position:

    • Place in center of vessel
    • Avoid touching vessel wall

    If placed incorrectly → inaccurate waveform.

  7. SWEEP SPEED

    Controls horizontal stretching of waveform.

    • Increase sweep → waveform spreads out (better detail)
    • Decrease sweep → more cardiac cycles visible

  8. INVERT (IF NEEDED)

    Used if waveform is displayed below baseline but you want it above.

QUICK SCAN ROOM CHECKLIST

If waveform looks wrong, ask:

  • Is my angle ≤ 60°?
  • Is PRF appropriate?
  • Is gain too high/low?
  • Is wall filter cutting low flow?
  • Is gate in center?
  • Is sweep speed, correct?

Example Scenarios

Carotid artery (stenosis)

  • Increase PRF
  • Angle ≤ 60°
  • Adjust baseline
  • Moderate wall filter

Portal vein

  • Lower PRF
  • Low wall filter
  • Increase gain slightly

COLOR DOPPLER

Color Doppler displays blood flow direction and velocity within a vessel. It is based on the Doppler Effect, where moving blood cells cause a frequency shift in returning ultrasound waves.

COLOR/POWER DOPPLER BOX

Is the box that populates onto the screen when color or power doppler is turned on. It displays the color doppler signals

COLOR MAP

s a color-coded map of the doppler shift and it is located on the ultrasound monitor when color doppler is switched on.

BASELINE

Is a black line that separates the positive doppler shift from the negative doppler shift. And it represents an area of zero doppler shift.

COLOR REPRESENTATIONS ON COLOR DOPPLER MAP

Color Meaning
Color above baseline Flow toward transducer
Color below baseline Flow away from transducer
Black Baseline
Brighter colors Higher velocity
Darker colors Lower velocity
Mosaic colors Mixed velocities (often aliasing)

Important: Always check the color map. Different ultrasound machines can reverse the colors.

COLOR INVERT CONTROL

It flips the color assigned to the positive and negative doppler shift on the color map.

NB. Positive doppler shift will always be above the baseline and negative doppler shift will be below the baseline

IMPORTANT RULE: Flow direction is determined by baseline position, not color. Always pay attention to what color is on top! NB. When sampling a vessel make the sample gate is not perpendicular to the vessel and also the gate is placed in the center of the vessel.

VELOCITY

Is the rate of blood flow. And can be measured at peak systole and end diastole, and or be measured as a mean value.

COLOR DOPPLER MACHINE CONTROLS

These are very important during scanning.

COLOR GAIN

A control on an ultrasound machine that amplifies or decreases the doppler shift signal.

If color gain is set too high there will be spurious echoes (noise) present within the color or power doppler box.

And if color gain is set too low decreased color or power doppler sensitivity. And blood flow information can be lost.

CORRECT WAY TO SET COLOR OR DOPPLER GAIN

  1. Turn the gain all the up until noise is displayed with the color or power doppler box
  2. Turn the gain control slowly down, just until the noise disappears

This is optimal color or power doppler gain setting (Sensitive enough to pick up flow, but low enough to avoid noise artifact).

VELOCITY SCALE (PRF SCALE)

Colors are assigned on the color doppler map to signify the different velocities. The further away from the baseline (in either direction), the higher the velocity of the blood flow. It increases or decreases the range of velocities that the machine can detect The velocities of the blood flow are represented on each end of the color map. And changed with the PRF/Scale on the ultrasound machine.

PULSE REPETITION FREQUENCY (PRF)

The number of pulses emitted per second. Higher PRF is needed for higher blood flow velocities and decreases sensitivity to slow flow.

NYQUIST LIMIT

The maximum Doppler shift that can be detected without aliasing (wraparound effect), which is equal to 1/2 of the PRF. OR Maximum velocity measurable without aliasing.

Nyquist = PRF / 2/

If velocity exceeds Nyquist: → Aliasing occurs.

COLOR DOPPLER ARTIFACTS

Appears as pixelated dots of color within the color or power doppler box due to color or doppler gain being set too high.

TYPES OF COLOR DOPPLER ARTIFACTS

FLASH (MOTION) ARTIFACT

Is a motion artifact that creates artifactual echoes within the power or color doppler box.

COLOR BLEED

Are artifactual echoes displayed in area where there should be no color echoes. And this occurs when the color or power doppler gain is set too high.

NOISE

Are artifactual color echoes within a color or power doppler box that do not correspond to actual blood flow.

CHARACTERISTICS OF VEINS

VEINS

Veins carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart. Normal venous flow has five characteristics:

  • Spontaneous
  • Non-pulsatile
  • Phasic
  • Augmentable
  • Compressible

VENOUS WAVEFORM

  • Phasic with respiration
  • Low velocity
  • Often below the baseline

Exceptions: Some veins near the heart show pulsatile waveforms.

Example: Portal vein.

DUPLEX & TRIPLEX ULTRASOUND IMAGING

Means using more than one ultrasound at the same time.

DUPLEX IMAGING

Using greyscale ultrasound imaging plus either spectral doppler, color doppler or power doppler used at the same time on an ultrasound screen.

TYPES OF DUPLEX IMAGING

  • B-mode + spectral Doppler
  • B-mode + color Doppler

TRIPLEX IMAGING

Using greyscale ultrasound imaging plus color doppler or power doppler plus spectral doppler used or displayed at the same time on an ultrasound screen.
