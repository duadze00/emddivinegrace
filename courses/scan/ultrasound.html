ULTRASOUND PHYSICS

Ultrasound is defined as sound with a frequency higher than the hearing limit of the human ear, i.e., above 16 to 20 kHz. Its utility as a diagnostic tool rests on understanding and manipulating multiple physics principles. Knowledge of ultrasound wave emission, interaction with fluid, tissue, varying densities, wave reception, and machine data processing are integral to its function

Ultrasound machines rely on different probe types to emit sound waves at various frequencies, depths, directions, focal points, and power levels, using motion and artifact management tools to enhance imaging. Understanding the physics of ultrasound is critical to optimizing, interpreting, and integrating clinical images. The American Institute of Ultrasound in Medicine recommends a standardized curriculum to educate clinical sonographers on both physics principles and clinical applications as in other imaging areas; physics is foundational to ultrasonic imaging, which uses high-frequency sound. Ultrasound for imaging is produced in special crystal materials that, when electrically excited, vibrate at millions of times per second. Transducers both generate and detect this sound. The ultrasonic energy is focused into a narrow beam directed into tissues. Along its path, the beam undergoes reflection, refraction, absorption, and scattering. These processes depend on the ultrasound frequency and the medium's physical properties

Reflected or scattered echoes return to the transducer and are converted into electrical signals displayed as images. Echo sizes and locations determine diagnostic parameters and image structure. Intensity varies with tissue type and depth. Strong reflectors, like gas-tissue interfaces, return most energy and block further propagation. Bone absorbs ultrasound, converting it to heat and casting acoustic shadows. In contrast, liquid cavities allow unimpeded transmission and serve as acoustic windows. Electronic compensation adjusts for echo variations at different depths.

Sound requires:

  1. A medium (cannot travel through a vacuum)
  2. Particle vibration
  3. Compression and rarefaction cycles

PROPERTIES OF SOUND WAVE

  1. FREQUENCY (f)

    Frequency in a sound wave refers to the rate of the vibration of the sound travelling through the air. This parameter decides whether a sound is perceived as high pitched or low pitched. The frequency of the vibrating source of sound is calculated in cycles per second.

    The SI unit for frequency is Hertz and its definition is 1/T where T refers to the time period of the wave. The time period is the time required for the wave to complete one cycle. The wavelength and frequency of a sound wave are related mathematically as: The velocity of Sound = Frequency / Wavelength

    Number of cycles per second (Hz).

    Higher frequencies (MHz) are used for superficial structures (high resolution, shorter wavelength, low penetration).

    Lower frequencies (MHz) are used for deep structures (low resolution, longer wavelength, high penetration).

  2. WAVELENGTH (λ)

    Distance of one cycle. It is inversely proportional to frequency (f).

    Velocity = Frequency x Wavelength

    λ = c / f

    Where:

    1. λ = wavelength
    2. c = speed of sound
    3. f = frequency

    So:

    1. Higher frequency → shorter wavelength
    2. Shorter wavelength → better axial resolution

  3. SPEED (c)

    Determined by the medium's stiffness and density. Average soft tissue speed is assumed to be 1540 m/s.

    But:
    1. Fat = ~1450 m/s
    2. Bone = ~4080 m/s
    3. Air = ~330 m/s

  4. PROPAGATION

    Waves travel through a medium (tissue) by causing particles to vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel.

    When sound enters tissue:

    1. Travels in straight line (in homogeneous medium)
    2. Changes direction at interfaces
    3. Loses energy

    Energy interactions:

    1. Reflection
    2. Refraction
    3. Scattering
    4. Absorption

  5. PERIOD (T)

    Time taken for one cycle (1/f).

  6. AMPLITUDE

    Height of the wave. It determines strength or intensity of the echo.

    Higher amplitude → brighter echo.

HOW ULTRASOUND IS PRODUCED

PIEZOELECTRIC EFFECT

Certain crystals produce sound when electricity is applied.

Inside the transducer are piezoelectric crystals.

Crystals convert electrical energy to sound and vice versa.

TWO PROCESSES OCCUR:

  • Reverse Effect

    Electric current → crystal vibrates → ultrasound produced. OR

    Electric → vibration → ultrasound

  • Direct Effect

    Returning echoes → crystal vibrates → electrical signal produced. OR

    Echo → vibration → electrical signal → image

This electrical signal becomes the image on the screen.

BACKING MATERIAL

PULSE PHYSICS

Ultrasound travels in pulses.

PULSE

Pulse means group of sound cycles.

PULSE DURATION

Means time from start to end of a pulse.

Formula

Pulse Duration = Cycles x Period

Short pulse duration → better resolution

PULSE REPETITION PERIOD (PRP)

This is the time between start of one pulse and the next.

PULSE REPETITION FREQUENCY (PRF)

Is the number of pulses sent per second.

Formula

PRF = 1 / PRP

High PRF → better Doppler measurements.

DUTY FACTOR

Percentage of time ultrasound is transmitting.

Formula

Duty Factor = Pulse Duration / PRP

Diagnostic ultrasound < 1%

Machine listens most of the time.

IMAGE RESOLUTION

RESOLUTION

Is the ability to distinguish between two separate structures.

TYPES OF RESOLUTION

  • AXIAL RESOLUTION (LONGITUDINAL)

    Ability to distinguish two structures parallel to the beam path. It is determined by Spatial Pulse Length (SPL). Shorter pulses (higher frequency, shorter wavelength) improve axial resolution.

    SPL = wavelength x number of cycles

    To improve axial resolution:

    • ↑ Frequency
    • ↓ Pulse duration
    • Damping material in transducer

    Key rule: Axial resolution = ½ SPL

  • LATERAL RESOLUTION (AZIMUTHAL)

    Ability to distinguish two structures side-by-side (perpendicular to the beam). It is determined by beam width; a narrower beam (best at the focus) improves lateral resolution.

    To improve:

    • Use focal zones
    • Use higher frequency
    • Narrow beam

    Remember: Lateral resolution is worse than axial resolution.

  • ELEVATIONAL RESOLUTION

    Also called slice thickness resolution. Is the third dimension of beam. Poor elevational resolution causes partial volume artifact.

  • TEMPORAL RESOLUTION

    Ability to accurately represent motion over time. It is measured in frame rate (Hz). Higher frame rates (better temporal resolution) are achieved by reducing depth or sector angle.

    Frame rate improves when:

    • ↓ Depth
    • ↓ Focal zones
    • ↓ Line density
    • Narrow sector width

    Very important in echocardiography

IMAGE ARTIFACTS

ARTIFACTS

They are alteration in images which does not represent the actual area of interest.

TYPES OF IMAGE ARTIFACT

SHADOWING (ACOUSTIC)

Dark bands behind strongly attenuating/reflecting structures (e.g., stones, bone).

ENHANCEMENT (ACOUSTIC)

Increased brightness (hyperechoic region) behind fluid-filled structures due to lower attenuation of the beam.

MIRROR IMAGE

An artifact where a structure is duplicated on the opposite side of a strong reflector (e.g., liver/diaphragm interface)

REVERBERATION

Multiple, equally spaced echoes caused by the beam bouncing back and forth between two parallel, highly reflective surfaces. EDGE SHADOWING: Due to refraction at curved surfaces. Common in cysts, gallbladder

BIOEFFECTS ALARA PRINCIPLE

Ultrasound is safe, but not risk-free and must follow the ALARA Principle.

ALARA PRINCIPLE

"As Low As Reasonably Achievable" meaning always use the minimum output power and lowest time required to obtain a diagnostic image.
